An Ode to
Thinking

Eshaan Pareek 24 April 2026 4 min read

I feel we are not "thinking" enough nowadays. At least I haven't been.

But aren't we productive?

With the advent of LLMs, we have become hyper-productive. Most of us are able to finish week-long tasks in a day with a sizable token budget. I have been getting assignments that used to be a Masters project five years ago (I complained about it for the first fourteen days, then got it done in 3 hours with codex on the last day).

When we're getting so much work done on a daily basis, it seems but natural to think everything's alright and get lost in the flow. But I want you to take a moment and think, when was the last time you thought deeply about a problem? The sort of deep where you walk around in silence for half an hour chewing at the problem, with no screen in your hand. If my hypothesis is correct, at least not in the past month.

The illusion of the extended brain

This is my first article ever, so I decided to give it a good name. I came up with a few, but rejected all of them. Realised AI could give me a list of 15 relevant names in 30 seconds, easy. Then I recalled what it is that I wanted to write about.

I had given a mere 2 minutes to thinking. After 4 failed names I had a browser tab open.

This is something I have been observing lately and what motivated me to write this article - we are becoming dependent on tools like AI to do our thinking for us. The moment a task demands us to think beyond our comfort zone, we are tempted to offload it to AI. While this gets the task done, and probably done "better" than we could do ourselves, I feel it is depriving us of one of the most dopamine-producing activities we humans can do - solving a problem via thinking hard.

If you prioritize time and getting tasks done over emotions and dopamine, I don't blame you for not agreeing with my views so far. However, I am convinved that even if your goal is to be the most productive person at your workplace in the next 5 years, you should care more about developing your thinking than becoming efficient at outsourcing your thinking to AI. The reason I say this is that, given the rate at which we are progressing, pretty soon the limit on what we can produce will be determined by the limits of our thought - the doing will be taken care of by tools.

I feel every task that we solve with AI on the front seat, no matter how neatly or timely done, is a potential loss. Menial tasks? definitely outsource. But AI should not deserve to make any ingenious decisions on your behalf. Had one done that task on their own, it would have costed them:

  • Time
  • Effort
  • Loss of comfort
  • Failures
  • Frustration
  • and many other negative feelings

And we know this. That is why we offload to AI. But what we often do not think about is the flip side, what could happen if we decided to tread into the uncomfortable, burn midnight oil figuring out every last detail, and see ourselves make it till the end. No AI, just your I.

  • Satisfaction and Joy
  • Deep understanding
  • New skills
  • Self-confidence
  • No guilt whatsoever

That said, it is very important to decide for which tasks the positives outweigh the negatives. If you need to get the project working before the presentation tomorrow, it is no time to be thinking about satisfction or fulfillment! Similarly if you want to build something without having learn some burdensome skills as pre-requisites, you might want to get AI to handle the un-interesting parts.

AI tells me I'm putting forth very strong opinions without justifying anything I say and this will make people mad. AI is usually right. So I'll give an example from my recent life.

I started my semester with a fresh problem statement for my BTech Project. It was my first stint at academic research, and I was excited to do a good job. However the 20,000-line SVF codebase has a way of frustrating anyone brave enough to try to understand what's happening. After a week of trying, I moved to AI. Immediately things began to flow; tasks began to get ticked off instead of piling. It felt great - how progress feels.

But as time progressed, a feeling of emptimess began to grow. I could no longer force a smile when my guide complimented my work during weekly progress meets. And the real problem was when users of my work found issues that I had no explanation for, or idea how to fix.

So when it came to my second problem statement, I firmly decided that I would not offload thinking work to an LLM. This one required an understanding of LLVM IR and optimization passes, which was again a steep learning curve. It was painful. Not rosy to watch. I walked around for hours in the corridors of my department thinking how to cover a particular case, or why a certain part was not working. Finally when I had all the details sketched out, only then did I use AI to get the coding and testing done. It worked. I can still vividly recall the moment that evening when my code successfully ran - that is how happpy I felt. I had had to request to cancel 2 weekly meetings due to no tangible progress. But I could finally smile a very wide smile when my guide told me I had done a fantastic job! What's more, I knew to the tiniest detail what was going on in the code, so I could confidently give guarantees on what could and what would not go wrong.

So what's the solution?

Based on what I said so far, you might (understandably) believe that I am strongly against the use of AI. However that is far from the truth; that would be like telling factories not to use machines. What we need to take care of is to sharpen our ability of thinking deeply. The process I have started following for my tasks has been quite rewarding, I encourage you to try it at least for a week, after adapting it to what feels good to you. When faced with a problem/task, I follow these steps:

  1. Decide whether the task is worth the time and effort that would go in thinking it through. If you are short on time, or the subject is not something you care to be good at, the answer is probably no. This evaluation won't always be easy; the best way is to go with "yes" if unsure.
  2. If your answer is yes: think, think, and think until the problem is crystal clear. Often the perception of a problem being too hard is what makes us give up on trying it ourselves, but once you think it through, it isn't really that hard.
  3. Think harder to come up with a solution. I know, easier said than done. This is probably the phase when you will be most tempted to give up and give in to AI. But trust me, the feeling when you finally solve it yourself will shadow all the pain the process gave. The best views require the hardest climbs.
It is completely fine to give in during any of these steps, don't blame yourself too hard if you do. Not all tasks need to be won over. The goal is to increasingly hone your ability to think critically, one task at a time.

Concluding remarks

I am myself far from perfect, in fact quite far. Through this article I do not mean to "educate" anyone on how to think more often. I merely wish to sensitize anyone who is harming their ability to think without realising it.

If this article interested you, do check out the book "Deep Work" by Cal Newport (it's available for free!).

As mentioned before, this is my first article. I'm delighted and pleasantly surprised that you made it to the end. I'd love to hear your thoughts on the topic or any feedback you might have! (bricks and bouquets equally welcome)
